Rosemary (Salvia rosmarinus)

Commonly known as rosemary, it is a shrub with fragrant, evergreen, needle-like leaves and colorful flowers.

Description

Rosemary has a fibrous root system and forms an aromatic evergreen shrub. The plant can reach heights of up to 1.8 meters (6 feet), with needle-like leaves and flowers that range in color from white to blue.

Taxonomy

Salvia rosmarinus was formerly placed in the genus Rosmarinus. The genus name was given by Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century.

Etymology

The botanical name "Rosmarinus" is derived from Latin, meaning "Sea Dew". Rosemary thrives best by the sea.

Distribution and Habitat

Native to the Mediterranean region, rosemary was brought to England by the Romans and later spread across the world. It is hardy in cooler climates and drought-resistant.

Cultivation

Rosemary is an ornamental plant, commonly used in gardens, especially in regions with Mediterranean climates. It is easy to grow and pest-resistant.

Cultivars

  • 'Arp': Cold-hardy, lemon-scented.
  • 'Blue Rain': Pink flowers.
  • 'Tuscan Blue': Upright form.
  • ... (continue list of cultivars)

Uses

Culinary

Rosemary leaves are commonly used in cooking for flavoring stews, meats, and marinades.

Fragrance

Rosemary essential oil is used in perfumes, shampoos, and aromatherapy.

Phytochemicals

The essential oil contains several phytochemicals such as camphor, rosmarinic acid, and carnosic acid.

Cultural Significance

Rosemary has been used in ceremonies and traditions, such as memorials and weddings, and is mentioned in literature by Shakespeare.
